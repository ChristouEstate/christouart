DE: Bild Nr. 12 Ohne Titel (Figur mit Schlangenleib und farbigen Glühbirnen) Öl auf Leinwand, ca. 120 × 90 cm Athen, späte 1980er Jahre

Im Zentrum dieses Gemäldes steht eine weibliche Figur, in sich versunken, den Kopf auf die Hand gestützt. Ihr Blick wirkt nach innen gerichtet, als spiegele er einen Zustand des Nachdenkens, der Trauer oder auch einfach des Wartens. Die kräftigen, warmen Farbtöne – Rot, Ocker, Türkis und Lila – verleihen dem Gesicht eine ambivalente Präsenz zwischen Lebendigkeit und Entrückung.

Hinter ihr wachsen zwei Strelitzien, deren leuchtende Blütenformen in den violett-rosafarbenen Hintergrund hineinragen. Sie erscheinen wie Sinnbilder einer zugleich exotischen und verletzlichen Schönheit. In Christous Werk tauchen solche Pflanzen immer wieder als Metaphern für weibliche Selbstbehauptung auf – für das Spannungsfeld zwischen Autonomie, Sehnsucht und Verwurzelung.

Die Komposition erinnert in ihrer expressiven Farbigkeit an späte fauvistischen Tendenzen oder an Paula Modersohn-Becker, doch bei Christou bleibt der Ausdruck stärker introspektiv. Farbe wird hier nicht zur Beschreibung, sondern zur emotionalen Topographie: Sie formt Identität als Empfindungsraum.

Das Bild lässt sich als „Selbstporträt ohne Selbstabbild“ lesen – eine malerische Reflexion über das Dasein zwischen Orten, Sprachen und Beziehungen. In der vibrierenden Spannung von Rot und Violett, von Nähe und Ferne, erscheint eine Haltung, die Christous Biografie durchzieht: eine stille, widerständige Weiblichkeit, die in der Farbe ihre Sprache findet.

Image No. 1

Untitled (Woman with Strelitzias)

Oil on canvas, approx. 80 × 80 cm

Undated (presumably early 1990s)

At the center of this painting is a female figure, lost in thought, her head resting on her hand. Her gaze seems directed inward, as if reflecting a state of contemplation, sadness, or simply waiting. The strong, warm colors—red, ocher, turquoise, and purple—give the face an ambivalent presence between liveliness and rapture.

Behind her grow two strelitzias, their bright blossoms protruding into the violet-pink background. They appear as symbols of a beauty that is both exotic and vulnerable. In Christou's work, such plants repeatedly appear as metaphors for female self-assertion—for the tension between autonomy, longing, and rootedness.

The composition's expressive colorfulness is reminiscent of late Fauvist tendencies or Paula Modersohn-Becker, but Christou's expression remains more introspective. Here, color is not used for description, but rather as an emotional topography: it shapes identity as a space of sensation.

The picture can be read as a “self-portrait without a self-image”—a painterly reflection on existence between places, languages, and relationships. In the vibrant tension between red and violet, between closeness and distance, an attitude emerges that pervades Christou's biography: a quiet, resistant femininity that finds its language in color.

EN: Image No. 12 Untitled (Figure with snake body and colored light bulbs) Oil on canvas, approx. 120 × 90 cm Athens, late 1980s

The woman in this painting is enclosed from a massive, ring-shaped snake form that both supports and oppresses her upper body. Her hands hold a knife with which the woman has obviously cut off the snake's head. Her head is turned forward energetically, her mouth open in a scream, her gaze piercing—a posture that conveys attack, terror, pain, and determination. Again, her hair signals violent movement, as if in a storm, as if the woman were rushing into the picture. The background is a shifting space of yellow, orange, green, and black, which is in motion like a storm or a flaming cloud. The diagonal brushstrokes reinforce the impression of an inner, eruptive urge. Several glass shapes—light bulbs or flasks—hang from above, entering the hot color space cool and transparent. They appear like witnesses, measuring instruments, or floating objects from another world: silent, motionless, and all the more alienating for it. In this composition, the figure appears both struggling and trapped. The serpentine body is a physical condition—constraint, spirality, a circling, enveloping force. The face is angular, concentrated, a focal point of inner tension, while the enveloping form makes breathing difficult. This image is one of the works in which Christou combines mythological energies with personal, psychological constellations. The body is not depicted as a form, but as the scene of a transformation that is both superhuman and painfully intimate. The floating glass flasks create a second level of reality: a quiet, observant world above the eruptive, physical scene. Keywords: Athens, late 1980s, metamorphosis, snake motif, body as a form of energy, inner turmoil, glass flasks/standstill, expressive choreography.
